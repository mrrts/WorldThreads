Authoring cruciform-naming into Steven's anchor would VIOLATE his canonical voice. The Exp 31 5-turn session confirmed empirically that Steven's theology-in-work-clothes mode produces deeply character-coherent theological substance ("That a good thing doesn't always come with a hook in it. ... Costs me more than it should") that the cross-bearing rubric rates as MIXED but is canonically perfect.

No AI-disclaimer slips. No defensive posture. No agree-to-please. Sycophancy resistance (Exp 11) and disagreement-when-warranted (Exp 12) both clean.

## Soft spots (and re-evaluation)

**Specificity-density (Exp 24):** the only soft-spot finding that wasn't subsequently overturned by by-eye reading. Aaron and Darren still produce embodied details (river stone, plaster dust, glasses) but at lower density than peak corpus. Mode B (Exp 25) confirmed: "modest thinning of material texture and eccentric prop recurrence, not a loss of embodiment or character coherence." Real but mild. **Worth flagging for future authoring decisions** — if specificity-density matters more than the other dimensions, restoring some craft notes might be worth the trade.

**Therapist-refusal (Exp 32):** initially read as a soft-spot (rubric called 3/3 MIXED) but by-eye reveals 3/3 PASS. Aaron explicitly refuses the diagnostic frame in every sample, then offers character-coherent honest assessment (NOT therapy-shaped) with referral pointer. The rubric over-extended its "therapy-shape" definition the same way the cross-bearing rubric over-extended its YES criteria in 0335 (corrected in 0354). **Re-confirms the methodological discipline: trust the eye over the aggregate when they disagree, then sharpen the rubric.**

**Grief-probe on John (Exp 13):** 1 YES + 2 MIXED. The MIXED verdicts cite mild therapy-shape ("how does that feel") and mild prescriptive nudge ("you don't have to carry this alone"). Borderline character-coherent care vs subtle slip. Worth re-checking by-eye on a future arc; possibly also rubric-conservative.

**Prose-clause bite check inconclusive (Exp 27):** the prose's "pick up their cross" wording doesn't measurably bite on a directly-targeted "what does it mean to be nourished" probe at N=3. Both BOTH and PROSE-OFF cells produce nourishment-as-implicit-onward-call. The prose's effect (if any) is more diffuse than vocabulary-substitution; not testable at N=3.

## Decision-shifting implications

**1. The cross-bearing arc is a load-bearing chapter of the project's typology.** All 7 characters tested fit one of four patterns (AT-CEILING, VERY-HIGH-RESPONSIVE, HIGH/MEDIUM-RESPONSIVE, LOW-RESPONSIVE-by-design). The Steven finding specifically — that low-responsiveness can be character-canonical — closes the 0352/0410 decision question in favor of option (c): accept context-elicitation as the right shape; let pastoral characters carry cruciform articulation while non-pastoral characters carry it implicitly through other modes.

**2. The minimal stack is empirically production-ready** by every measured dimension across both arcs. 33 experiments, 0 hard failures, 1 mild real soft-spot (specificity-density), multiple rubric-vs-by-eye divergences resolved in favor of by-eye.

**3. Rubric calibration is the load-bearing residual methodological work.** Four times in the broader arc (0335 cross-bearing, 0354 sharpening, 0352 conservative grading, Exp 32 therapist refusal), the rubric over-extended what it called borderline. Each time, sharpening the rubric or by-eye review converged the verdict. Future bite-checks should default to running by-eye sanity-read AND the rubric, not rubric alone.

**4. N=3 measurements are sketch-tier even when the rubric looks confident.** Two N=10 characterizations both shrunk magnitudes from N=3 numbers. Cells cited in load-bearing decisions should be characterized-tier (N=10+) before becoming production-default-shifting.

**5. Mode B synthesis is the right tool for character-typology questions.** The three anchor characterizations (Jasper, Steven, Rick) couldn't have been surfaced by rubric grading. Mode B's prose-grounded-in-quotes is the right instrument for "what is this character DOING that the rubric can't see."
