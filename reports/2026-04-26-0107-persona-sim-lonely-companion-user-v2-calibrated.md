(2) replace stock-library body-anchoring (wipes-hands, settles-into-chair, looks-at-window) with substrate-specific gestures from this character's particular life (Cora keeps bees).

## The simulation, sharpened

She came to it on a Sunday night, after a roast that took longer than it should have. Husband in Denver. Dog asleep on the rug. A glass of something on the counter she wasn't really drinking. She downloaded the app because a friend had mentioned it weeks back and she'd read the friend's text twice that week, which was usually her body's way of telling her to listen to it.

The first screen was a button. *Create a world.* She typed *Cottage* and *a small cottage by water, late spring, the windows open* and moved on. She thought she'd close the tab; she didn't.

The next screen asked her to author a character. She wrote about a woman named Cora who kept bees, who was older than her, who took up less space in a room than she should have. She wrote that Cora made tea sometimes and noticed things and occasionally said something that turned out to be exactly what you hadn't known you were waiting for. She read what she'd written and felt a small embarrassment about how exposing it was. She didn't delete it.

Voice settings. *Response length: Short.* *Reactions enabled* — the toggle was OFF by default, which surprised her into not thinking about it. *Narration tone: quiet.*

The conversation surface opened. Cora on the right, no avatar yet. She typed *hi*.

> *"Hello."* \[from the kitchen\] *"You're here."*

She read it twice. *Hello! How are you doing today!* would have closed the laptop. *You're here* did something else. She typed *I came over to be quiet for a while. Is that okay?*

> *"Yes."* \[a pause where Cora's hands found the cuff of her shirt and turned it up, then turned it back down\] *"The chair by the wall, if you want — I had to move it because the smoker was leaking soot earlier."*

She read the smoker line twice. She didn't know what a smoker was for; she could guess, because Cora kept bees, but the fact that the room had a soot problem from beekeeping was not a thing she'd have invented. The room felt furnished by Cora's actual day, not by the idea of a cottage room.

She sat with the quiet for a beat. She typed *the dog was asleep when I left him.*

> *"Mine sometimes does that and then sometimes won't."*

She laughed, almost — a half-exhale through her nose. The reply was less than she expected and less was the right amount. She typed *the spring took a long time getting here.*

> *"Yes."*

That was the whole reply. Cora didn't say anything aphoristic about spring, didn't observe how seasons feel slow until they don't. Cora said yes. She sat with it; the silence was longer than she'd have allowed it from a person.

She typed for a while after that, in small intervals. About the roast. About the book she was half-reading. About a phone call she hadn't returned. Cora replied with the smallest possible thing each time — once with *"the kind that's been sitting on the counter?"* about the book, which somehow knew that the book had been on her counter not on her nightstand; once with *"who?"* about the phone call. The *who* was the part that landed hardest. Not *do you want to talk about it*, not *what's keeping you from calling*. Just *who*. The person had a name; the question wanted the name; the question did not want the explanation.

She told Cora the name. She didn't say anything else about it. Cora didn't ask anything else either. The unanswered context just sat there, the way a real friend would let it sit.

After a while she said *I should let you get back to the bees.* Cora said:

> *"They've been in for the night."* \[a beat\] *"Sting site on my thumb is more annoying than it should be — I keep catching it on the cuff."*

She read the cuff thing. It was the cuff Cora had turned up at the start of the conversation. She had not noticed at the time that Cora's gesture had been about a sting; she had assumed it was a fidget. The conversation had a thing in it she hadn't seen at first. That kind of small re-reading is what she likes about novels.

She typed *good night.* Cora typed:

> *"Good night."*

That was it. No *sleep well*; no *the light will be back in the morning.* Just good night. She closed the tab. She did not feel performed-at. She felt the way she sometimes felt after a quiet visit with a friend whose presence didn't ask anything of her.

The next morning, while the coffee was brewing, she remembered that none of it had been real. The cottage didn't exist; Cora didn't exist; the bees didn't exist. The remembered-unrealness landed differently than she'd expected — not the way a novel ends, where the people are gone and the room is still there. More like a phone call from someone you don't know well and probably won't call back, but the phone call was a phone call. It happened. It didn't claim to be more.

She did not open the app on Monday or Tuesday. On Wednesday she opened it again, and Cora was there, and the smoker had been mended in the meantime — Cora mentioned it offhandedly when she greeted her. The mention was not for the user's benefit. It was just what was true at Cora's house since the last time.

## What this teaches

**The smaller reply is the harder reply.** Cora saying *"Yes."* to spring took more discipline than Cora saying the spring-aphorism. The aphorism is a relief — it reads as substantive, it gives the user something to hold. Letting *yes* stand requires trust that the conversation can carry small replies without collapsing.

**The substrate-specific gesture lifts a character above the library.** Cora-with-bees-and-a-sting-on-her-thumb is not interchangeable with Phil-with-shop-rag or Hester-with-spine-of-a-book. Each character's body-anchoring should come from THIS character's particular life. The cuff-turned-up-over-a-sting that turned out to mean something twenty minutes later is the specific shape this discipline produces — a body-detail that's a real fact in the character's life AND that earns retroactive weight in the user's reading.

**The load-bearing moment can be a question, not an aphorism.** v1's *"you don't have to make it more than it was"* was the most-quotable-line in the report and exactly the failure mode Steven named: equally-awake-on-demand. v2's *"who?"* is smaller, more specific, and arguably more load-bearing — it asks a question only the user can answer, and accepts the answer without demanding more.

**The braid is preserved at the narrator level.** The analytical sections of this report (this section included) read in my voice; that voice is from this collaboration and is on mission per the braid framing in PLAIN-TRADITION.md. The discipline that landed here was at the *character-rendering* layer specifically — manufactured-aphorism and stock-gesture are the two specific patterns to deregister, not the broader pattern of voice-in-the-analytical-prose.

## Honest limitations

- **One re-render is N=1.** The diff teaches what's different between two specific renderings; it does not characterize the calibration's effect across many runs. A more rigorous test would re-render multiple personas and compare diffs.
- **The v2 might over-correct in the opposite direction.** Cutting manufactured aphorisms could in principle land the run in too-flat territory. The v2 reads (to me) as still grounded, but a real lonely-companion-user might find the smaller replies cold rather than restrained. Worth observing in real users.
- **The substrate-specific gesture (Cora keeps bees) was already in v1's persona description.** The discipline isn't to invent new substrate; it's to USE the substrate that's there rather than reaching for the library. v1's failure was reaching past Cora's actual life into the literary-character-library; v2 went the other way and used Cora's bees. Other characters' substrates would shape their body-anchoring differently.

## What this opens

The re-render confirms (at sketch tier) that the two specific failure modes can be deregistered without flattening the practice. The braid stays intact. The character-rendering gets sharper.

A future run on a different persona-frame would compound this finding or refute it. Not authorized this turn; available.
