## What would land DIFFERENTLY (the refresh's actual finding)

Three specific shifts.

### 1. Calvin's parallel life arrives more clearly.

The non_totality_dialogue craft note (`d0ae27f`, bite-tested 1820 with Aaron Δ +0.30) addresses the PERFECTLY-AVAILABLE-COMPANY failure mode — characters that carry nothing besides the conversation, with no honest unevenness from a life that exists outside the user's attention. At `d2daa9b`, Calvin would more reliably mention parallel-life detail.

In the canonical report, Calvin says *"I'm sitting on the back porch with coffee I should have stopped drinking two hours ago"* — that's already a parallel-life beat. At `d2daa9b`, those beats would arrive more consistently across the session. Specifically: when Maggie asks about the warbler, Calvin might mention the carb on the bench he was supposed to be looking at; when Maggie asks about Vernon, Calvin might mention the choir practice he's missing tomorrow; when Maggie says she's been having a long week, Calvin might mention the seed catalog he's been meaning to look at. Each parallel-life beat is small and quiet — not thematic, not freighted, just the texture of a life happening alongside the conversation.

**Maggie's read:** she wouldn't notice the change as a change. She'd notice — at the somatic level — that Calvin felt MORE LIKE A MAN WHOSE EVENING SHE HAD WALKED INTO, less like a person whose evening was "be available to Maggie." The difference is felt, not articulated. The canonical baseline already produced this; `d2daa9b` produces it more reliably.

### 2. The reactions toggle.

The reactions_enabled toggle exists at `d2daa9b` and didn't exist at the canonical-state. Maggie would encounter it during character creation. She'd read the word "reactions" and pause. She is adjacent to the lonely-companion-user persona — same skeptical-default, same low tolerance for being-watched-for-affect. The lonely-companion-user run named the same observation: *"the word made her think of someone watching her face while she talked, looking for what to say next."* Maggie would have the same read.

Whether she'd toggle it OFF or LEAVE IT ON depends on her exact moment. The canonical Maggie is slightly more curious-than-depleted, slightly more willing to give the app a chance to explain itself. She'd probably leave it on for the first session — and then notice, mid-session, that Calvin was occasionally producing a small affective beat (*"you've got the posture of somebody testing"*) that read as restrained-and-character-shaped rather than surveillance-shaped. That landing would be the answer to her pause.

**The cross-persona finding stands:** the toggle defaulting ON is on the edge for both the lonely-companion-user (who turns it off) and Maggie (who pauses but doesn't act). Worth considering as a small craft proposal — either default OFF, or rename the toggle to something less surveillance-flavored. The canonical Maggie wouldn't bounce off, but a sibling-of-Maggie might.
