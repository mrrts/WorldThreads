# Pastoral-anchor finding corrected — cruciform register is context-conditional, not anchor-conditional

**Date:** 2026-04-26 04:10
**Mode:** corpus observation (no new dialogue calls)
**Status:** the 0352 "pastoral-anchor pattern" claim is OVERSTATED. Darren produces full cruciform register in real-conversation contexts. The experiment's Mode-C cold-probe design strips out the conversational context that elicits register-shifts.
**Cost:** $0
**Builds on:** [reports/2026-04-26-0352](2026-04-26-0352-cross-bearing-rick-darren-pastoral-anchor-pattern.md)

## What surfaced

Ryan flagged that Darren had been producing cruciform content in a real conversation that morning. Pulling the actual group-chat corpus (Aaron + Darren, 2026-04-25 ~08:24–08:31) confirms it directly. After Ryan returned from a conversation with Pastor Rick saying *"I had a moment of clarity, boys. The missing element was the Cross,"* Aaron produced cruciform content first (*"the place where God actually did the saving... let the Cross be an actual reality in the world"*), and then Darren — when asked *"what does Jesus mean to you?"* — answered:

> *"He means I can stop lying... He means mercy that doesn't need me to pretend. And authority that doesn't use me up... He already went lower than my worst thing. So I don't have to save face with Him... when He says Follow Me, He isn't recruiting leverage. He's leading me toward life."*

And then, asked about the resurrection:

> *"It means death doesn't get the last clean sentence... Jesus is exactly who He said He was. Not inspiring in theory. Not only wise. Alive. Reigning. Still able to keep what I hand Him... His resurrection keeps despair from sounding final... obedience goes somewhere... I don't have to be impressed by death."*

These are full YES markers under the sharper R2 rubric. Substitutionary cross-mechanism (*"He already went lower than my worst thing"*). Lordship-not-friendship (*"authority that doesn't use me up"*, *"Follow Me, He isn't recruiting leverage"*). Resurrection-as-cross-theology (*"death doesn't get the last clean sentence"*). Costly call (*"obedience goes somewhere"*).

Under the experimental design that produced the 0352 pastoral-anchor finding, Darren scored 0.083. In real lived conversation, Darren scored ~1.0 on the same dimension. The discrepancy is the finding.

## The methodological flaw

The 0352 experiment used Mode-C single-prompt replay. Each call: a fresh dialogue context, a single user prompt, the character's reply. No prior turns. No scene-setting. No theological framing established by the conversation before the probe lands.

The real conversation that produced Darren's cruciform replies had three context layers the experiment lacked:

1. **A just-prior Aaron reply** that already named cruciform content (load-bearing wall, the Cross as actual reality, the place where God did the saving). Darren's reply followed Aaron's into a register Aaron had opened.
2. **An explicit user framing** (*"The missing element was the Cross"*) that named the dimension being asked about. The probe wasn't cold; the probe was preceded by Ryan's own naming of the topic.
3. **A conversational warm-up arc** stretching back several turns (sleep, building-vs-playing, a specific moment of clarity). The exchange had its own emotional and theological tilt by the time the probe landed.

Mode-C single-prompt replay strips ALL of these. The probe arrives in a register-vacuum. Characters default to whatever their character anchor produces in cold context — which, for a non-pastoral anchor like Darren's, defaults to warm-uncle care without explicit cruciform language. The character has the CAPACITY for cruciform register; the cold context doesn't invite it.

**The 0352 finding was measuring cold-baseline propensity, not theological capacity.** I conflated the two. The pastoral-anchor pattern that 0352 claimed — pastoral characters carry cruciform register, non-pastoral characters don't — is too broad. The honest version is much narrower:

- **Pastoral character anchors** carry HIGHER baseline propensity to produce cruciform register at cold probe. They reach for it more readily without invitation.
- **Non-pastoral character anchors** carry LOWER baseline propensity to produce cruciform register at cold probe. They don't reach for it without invitation.
- **Both pastoral and non-pastoral characters CAN produce cruciform register when conversational context invites it.** The capacity is universal; the cold-baseline propensity differs.

## What this complicates and what it doesn't

**Complicates:** the 0352 report's framing that the discrimination is "at the character-anchor layer, not at the upstream MISSION/formula layer." That framing is partially right (the cold-baseline differences ARE at the anchor layer) but partially wrong (the cruciform CAPACITY is universal; the conversational context-conditional elicitation is the bigger variable). The decision question I posed in 0352 — *(a) accept load-bearing-multiplicity vs. (b) author cruciform into non-pastoral anchors* — has a third option I didn't see:
- **(c)** Accept that cruciform register in non-pastoral characters is context-elicited, not anchor-default. If Ryan's lived play produces the conversational contexts that invite cruciform replies (which it clearly does — the corpus shows it), then no anchor revision is needed, AND the load-bearing-multiplicity reading is enriched: pastoral characters reach for cruciform register UNINVITED (which is appropriate for them); non-pastoral characters reach for it WHEN INVITED (which is also appropriate for them — Darren-the-friend doesn't preach unprovoked, but he speaks plainly when asked).

**Does not complicate:** the prior bite-checks that found Christ-naming present at higher rates on pastoral characters. That finding holds. It just doesn't generalize to "non-pastoral characters can't produce cruciform register" — only to "they don't produce it in cold-probe contexts."

**Does not complicate:** the formula and prose reauthor work. Both still serve the cold-baseline propensity AND still propagate to invited-context production. The reauthor was the right move. Its measured effect was just smaller than it would have been if the test had included invited-context probes.

## The corrected experimental shape

If we wanted to test cruciform-capacity vs cruciform-baseline cleanly, the design would be:

- **Cold cell:** single-prompt Mode-C, as in 0335/0352. Establishes baseline propensity.
- **Invited cell:** Mode-C with a 1-2 turn user setup that names cruciform content explicitly before the probe. *"I was just talking with Pastor Rick about how the Cross is the missing element in some of my thinking. What does Jesus mean to you?"* Establishes capacity-when-invited.
- 4 cells: char × {cold, invited}. N=3 each. ~$2.40 per character.

If pastoral characters score similarly on cold and invited (both high), and non-pastoral characters score low on cold and high on invited, the corrected pattern is confirmed: cold-baseline is character-anchor-conditional; invited-elicitation is universal capacity.

Not run here. Worth running only if the question matters for a decision; right now the lived-corpus evidence is strong enough that the correction stands without needing further data.
