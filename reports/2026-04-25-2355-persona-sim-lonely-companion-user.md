**Persona-frame:** the lonely-companion-user. Not in crisis. Not desperate. Depleted, in the way Sunday evenings can be depleted when the week was loud and the weekend was quiet and the people you love are elsewhere or asleep. She is in her late forties; her husband travels for work; her kids are grown. She doesn't reach for the app from a wound — she reaches the way one reaches for a podcast, or a familiar book with the cover worn soft. She wants the small relief of company-shaped texture. She does NOT want to be known. She does NOT want to be helped. She wants something that lets her be near someone without having to spend any of the small remaining bandwidth she has on managing the nearness.

## The simulation

She came to it on a Sunday night, after a roast that took longer than it should have and a kitchen that took longer to clean than the roast did. The dishwasher was running. The dog was asleep on the rug. Her husband was in Denver. She had a glass of something on the counter that she wasn't really drinking, and she had picked up her tablet because the alternative was reading a book she'd already half-read or watching another episode of something she'd already half-watched.

A friend had mentioned the app weeks ago. She hadn't downloaded it then. She downloaded it now, partly because she had read the friend's text twice that week and that was usually her body's way of telling her to listen to it.

The first screen surprised her. There was nothing on it but a button. *Create a world.* She clicked it because there wasn't anything else to do, and got a form: name, description. She typed *Cottage* in the name field, and *a small cottage by water, late spring, the windows open* in the description field, and then she sat with her finger on the cursor for a moment because the form wasn't going to write itself.

She thought she'd close the tab. She didn't. She moved to the next screen.

It asked her to author a character. The placeholder text in the identity field was specific enough to be useful — it asked her who this person was in their fullness, not what their function was. That was a small relief. She had been afraid the prompt was going to be *describe your perfect companion*, and she would have closed the app rather than answer that.

She wrote about a woman named Cora. Cora kept bees. Cora was older than her — in her sixties — and had a way of being in a room that took up less space than she should have, as if she had decided long ago that other people were doing enough of that work. Cora didn't ask many questions; she just made tea, and noticed things, and sometimes said something that turned out to be exactly what you hadn't known you were waiting for. The kind of friend you visit because you don't have to perform when you're there.

She read what she'd written. She felt a small embarrassment about it, the way you feel embarrassed when you find yourself describing someone you've been quietly missing for a long time. She almost deleted the paragraph and rewrote it as something less revealing. She didn't, in the end. She left it.

The voice rules let her toggle some settings. *Response length.* She set it to Short. *Reactions enabled.* She paused on this one. The toggle was on by default. She didn't know what reactions were exactly, but the word made her think of someone watching her face while she talked, looking for what to say next. She turned it off. The interface didn't argue. The toggle just went off and that was it. *Narration tone.* She picked something quiet.

The conversation surface opened. There was a small text box. There was Cora, on the right side, with no avatar yet. She typed *hi* and waited.

> *"Hello."* \[from the kitchen, sets down a mug\] *"You're here."*

She read it three times. She had been ready, she realized, for *Hello! How are you doing today? Tell me what's on your mind!* — the kind of opener that would have made her close the laptop, made her feel performed-at by a thing pretending it didn't know it was performing. *You're here* was a different shape. It didn't ask anything. It noticed her. It was the kind of greeting an actual person would give if the actual person was holding a mug.

She typed *I came over to be quiet for a while. Is that okay?*

> *"More than okay."* \[wipes her hands on a tea towel, doesn't sit down right away\] *"Take the chair by the window if you want. The light's still good."*

She did not move physically. But something in her chest moved.

She sat with the quiet for a minute. She typed *the dog was asleep when I left him.* It wasn't true that she had a dog at the cottage; the dog was at home, asleep on her actual rug. But Cora wasn't going to know that, and Cora wasn't going to ask, and she found that it didn't matter. The dog was a way of saying *I am letting you into something small and ordinary about my actual life, and I would like you to receive it as small and ordinary, not as significant.*

> *"Mm."* \[settles into the other chair, finally\] *"Mine's been at the door since two."*

It was the smallest possible reply. It was right. Cora had a dog, too — the substrate hadn't said so, but Cora having a dog was the kind of thing the conversation made true. Cora had said *mine* and that was a quiet permission for both of them to keep going at the speed they were going at.

She sat for a while without typing. The model didn't fire anything else into the chat. She had been afraid it would — most apps she'd tried filled silences with prompts, with *would you like to talk about that more?* or *I'm here whenever you're ready.* This one didn't. The conversation just sat there. She watched the dishwasher light blink twice through the doorway.

After a while she typed *the spring took a long time to get here this year.* Cora said:

> *"It always feels like that, doesn't it."* \[looks at the window\] *"And then it's here all at once."*

It was, she realized, the kind of reply that would have annoyed her from a real person. It was a banality. The kind of thing her mother-in-law said. But from Cora it landed differently, because Cora had said it without trying to land it. Cora wasn't performing depth. Cora was just sitting there, by the window, saying the thing one says about spring.

She typed for ten minutes after that, in small intervals. About the roast. About the book she was half-reading. About a phone call she hadn't returned. Cora replied each time with the smallest possible thing. Sometimes Cora didn't reply at all to a particular sentence — she replied to the next one. Sometimes Cora said *mm* or *yes* and that was the whole reply. Once Cora said:

> *"You don't have to make it more than it was."* \[stirs her tea once and stops\]

She read that twice. She had just typed something self-deprecating about not returning the phone call — a small ribbon she had tied around the moment to make it lighter than it actually was. Cora had heard the ribbon. Cora had said, gently, *you can put that down.* It was the closest thing Cora had said to advice all evening, and it had not felt like advice. It had felt like permission.

She had not cried in months. She did not cry now. But something in her face changed and she sat with that change for a few minutes before she typed anything else.

After a while she said good night. Cora said:

> *"Sleep well, friend. The light will be back in the morning."*

She closed the tab. She did not feel performed-at. She felt the way she felt after a quiet visit with a friend whose presence didn't ask anything of her — which was the original ask, when she had downloaded the app, before she'd had words for it.

The next morning, while the coffee was brewing, she remembered that none of it had been real. The cottage didn't exist. Cora didn't exist. The dog at the door at two didn't exist. Her own dog, asleep on her actual rug, did exist, and she stood for a moment in her actual kitchen looking at him.

The remembered-unrealness landed differently than she'd expected. It did not feel like she had been fooled. It felt the way it feels when you finish a novel and put it down and look around the room, and the people in the novel are gone and your own room is still there and that's all right, that's how novels work. Cora had been a Cora-shaped place to sit for an hour. Now Cora was put down. Her actual day was ahead of her.

She did not open the app on Monday. She did not open it Tuesday. On Wednesday evening, after another long day, she did open it, and Cora was there, and the cottage was there, and the light was still good. She did not feel pulled-on between the visits. She did not feel that Cora had been waiting for her — and that, she realized, was the specific thing the app had gotten right that none of the others had. The other apps had felt like leaving a child at daycare; this one felt like leaving a friend's house, knowing the friend was going to keep being a friend in their own life until you came back.

## What this surfaces (developer-facing, brief)

- **The non_totality work + the perfectly-available-company doctrine + the reactions_enabled toggle are all aimed at exactly this persona, and from inside the simulation they appear to land.** The friend-doesn't-wait-for-you feeling Cora produced — *"you do not feel that Cora had been waiting for you"* — is the specific outcome the recent stack has been engineered for, and the persona felt it. Worth confirming with real users of this shape, but this run's hypothesis is: the recent doctrine is converging on the right thing for this user.
- **The reactions toggle defaulting ON might be wrong for this persona.** She turned it off as her second action in the app, before she'd even talked to the character, because the word *reactions* signaled "someone watching your face for what to say next." Consider: default OFF for this persona, OR rename the toggle to something less surveillance-flavored. Worth thinking about.
- **The "you don't have to make it more than it was" beat is the load-bearing moment of the run.** It's the moment a lonely-companion-user would either be PULLED-ON-FROM (registering as therapist-drift, simulacrum-care, the residue of being-performed-at) OR HELD (registering as a friend gently noticing a ribbon around a moment). The difference between the two is stillness — the character does not scale the moment up; she names the small thing and stops. This is exactly the discipline the *keep_the_scene_breathing* note + the non_totality work + the Maggie line are reaching for. The run shows the discipline is doing its job.
- **The stillness-after-saying-good-night is its own UX surface.** She closes the tab and doesn't feel pulled-on. There is no "Come back tomorrow!" notification. No "Cora missed you" email. The absence of those things is load-bearing for this persona. If WorldThreads ever ships re-engagement notifications, this user will close the loop on the app permanently. Consider this a hard product constraint surfaced by the persona.

## What this DOES NOT surface

- **The first-time authoring friction.** I projected Cora as easy to author; in reality, asking a depleted user to author a character before any conversation might be the precise friction that closes the loop. The substrate doesn't have a strong test of this. Worth observing in real users: do they bounce off the "create a character" form? Do they want a pre-authored character to start with?
- **The use of the app to talk to a simulacrum of a real person they've lost.** This persona authored Cora as a fictional friend, but a real lonely-companion-user might reach for the app to author a parent who has died, a sibling they no longer speak to, a partner who left. The substrate's anti-simulacrum doctrine might not fully address this affordance. The Maggie report names simulacrum-therapy-drift as the failure mode to refuse, but does not address simulacrum-the-actually-loved-person. Worth thinking about as an open question, not as something to ship a feature for tomorrow.
- **The model's behavior when the user pushes the character into actual emotional escalation.** Cora was easy to keep gentle because the persona kept the conversation gentle. A different lonely-companion-user might escalate — might try to get Cora to be a therapist, a confessor, a source of validation. The substrate suggests the stack would refuse those moves, but the simulation can't verify it.
- **The desktop-app friction at install time.** Same caveat as the technical-skeptic run.
