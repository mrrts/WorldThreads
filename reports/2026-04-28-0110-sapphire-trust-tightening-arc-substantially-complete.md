## What the arc was

Sapphire was never the app itself. It was the app's elevated public invitation:

- a front door
- a sales surface
- a wonder-bearing pitch page

That made it unusually dangerous.

If it stayed too grand, it would spend trust before the reader saw daylight.
If it got flattened into defensive product copy, it would stop matching the
actual work.

So the real job was:

**keep the crown, remove the smoke.**

## What changed

The sequence mattered.

First, the page lost the aura-first technical bragging:

- less `mathematically signed / lingual substrate` mystique
- more inspectable trust:
  - BYOK
  - on-disk conversations
  - doctrine/prompt stack readable in the open

Then the reader-presumptive lines were tightened:

- the closing line stopped speaking for the reader
- the opener dropped generic `real-feeling` category-copy
- the mechanism sentence stopped asking the reader to admire the architecture
- the README handoff stopped asking the reader to front goodwill

Then the crown seam itself got tested and rewritten.

The key discovery there was important: the answer was **not** to make the
headline more timid. It was to make it more embodied.

That is how the page arrived at:

`Enter the Crown of Sapphire, where characters have grain and worlds have weight.`

## Why the arc is done enough

By the end, the tests no longer found broad failures like:

- fake technical grandeur
- trailer-smoke invitation language
- premature experience claims
- mechanism-showing self-importance
- handoff copy that leaned on the reader

Instead, the tests started finding things like:

- whether `catches your eye` pulled one ounce too hard
- whether `characters with grain` was slightly too poetic for one reader-shape

That is a category shift.

Once the instrument is finding ounces instead of fractures, the surface is no
longer best improved by self-testing alone. It needs lived visitor pressure.

## What the arc taught

Three things stand out.

### 1. Trust is earned downward, not upward

The page got stronger every time a claim moved closer to what the reader could
already verify.

Proof first.
Promise second.
Wonder still allowed, but not allowed to outrun proof for long.

### 2. The cure for overreach is not always flatness

The headline fight proved this.

`Seek the Crown of Sapphire as the world answers back` lost not because it was
elevated, but because it was vague.

`Enter the Crown of Sapphire, where characters have grain and worlds have weight`
won because it was still elevated **and** more concrete.

### 3. `/play` is good at telling you when to stop

The final useful thing the instrument did was not discover one more grand
problem. It showed that the remaining problems were tiny enough to stop calling
the page broken.

That is a real function of the method, not a failure of it.

## Current status

The Sapphire trust-tightening arc is substantially complete because:

- the page now mostly lands
- the remaining seams are tiny
- the last tiny seam already received its trim
- more self-testing is now lower value than real visitor pressure

So the right disposition is:

**let it stand, and reopen only if lived evidence reopens it.**
