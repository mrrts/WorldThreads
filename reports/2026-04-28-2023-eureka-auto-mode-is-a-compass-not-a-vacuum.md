# /eureka — Auto mode is a compass, not a vacuum

*Generated 2026-04-28 after reading the live response-length seam in `prompts.rs` alongside older doctrine about "getting out of the way" on Auto. Names the refined prompt-stack law: opting out of hard length contracts does not require opting into shapelessness.*

## Discovery

The discovery is: **Auto mode is a compass, not a vacuum**.

The prompt-stack seam that surfaced it is specific:

- `Short`, `Medium`, and `Long` are explicit commandment-modes
- `Auto` is not one more commandment-mode
- but neither is `Auto` best understood as "no shape at all"

That last distinction is the real discovery.

Earlier work had already correctly rejected a bad Auto move: slipping a full sermon or disguised constraint into Auto and pretending that still counted as honoring the user's opt-out. That rejection remains right. But the live seam shows the equal-and-opposite mistake more clearly now:

> treating Auto as a total vacuum where the stack must say nothing at all, even if local drift and prompt mass quietly push the replies longer than the healthy equilibrium

The refined law is narrower and truer:

- **remove hard contracts and early commandment-language**
- **permit a light late-slot brevity compass**
- **keep the default lean unless the moment genuinely needs more air**

## Why this is not a contradiction

It might sound at first like a reversal of the old "get out of the way" line. It isn't.

The earlier correction was against **pseudo-Auto**: a setting labeled Auto that still behaved like hidden Medium through early prompt pressure and verbose variety-sermons.

The live correction is against **empty-Auto**: a setting labeled Auto that abdicates all late restraint and lets drift or accumulated prompt mass decide the length accidentally.

So the refined distinction is:

- **Too strong:** Auto becomes disguised contract
- **Too weak:** Auto becomes shapeless drift

The healthy middle is:

- **Auto as a compass**

That means no hard cap, no obedience-sermon, no fake "law of the chat" rhetoric — but still a late reminder that the default register is lean, load-bearing, and not obligated to fill the available air.

## Why this matters

This belongs with the project's broader anti-fake-shape doctrines:

- register is not mood-performance
- trust is not aura-performance
- shipment UX proof is dimension-bounded

Same family, different seam. Here the fake shapes are:

- a surface that says "Auto" while secretly behaving like a contract mode
- a surface that says "Auto" while quietly abandoning any healthy default shape

The doctrine refuses both.

## Practical test

The test is simple:

> If Auto guidance reads like a commandment, it is too heavy. If Auto routinely swells from lack of any late restraint, it is too empty.

That gives the prompt-stack a clean middle path:

- commandment for the explicit contract modes
- compass for the non-contract mode

## Outcome

The response-length family is now more sharply readable:

- `Short` / `Medium` / `Long` are **contracts**
- `Auto` is a **compass**

That is a better prompt-stack truth than either extreme. It honors the user's opt-out from hard constraint without pretending that healthy default shape must therefore disappear.
