## The day's two named shifts

The 0035 report tracked the apparatus growing recursive. The day shipped two specific refactors that are best read as *the apparatus catching itself in a drift and correcting structurally*. Both fit Ryan's "philosophical shift" cadence trigger — both can be named in a sentence.

The first is **doctrine and classifier converge when the classifier reads each reply on its own terms**. This sounds abstract; the worked example is concrete. Codex's stress-pack grading infrastructure (24-probe pass rate, two-signal gate, surprise-safe rubric update) had been calibrating itself across multiple commits on April 29 morning — `113f2b9a` shipped *do not penalize surprise/variety by default* into both the rubric body and a new threshold-band analysis. But the same infrastructure ran on top of `chat-improvement-loop.sh`'s python `is_pass()` classifier, whose verb-list reflected an earlier and less calibrated reading of what counted as "actionable." Cursor's micro-followup at `974409c1` introduced a `dimension_candidate` label as a third tier between `strict_pass` and `true_miss` — a useful diagnostic that lived in one JSON artifact without entering the canonical pipeline. The doctrine layer protected variety; the rubric layer named "do not penalize variety"; the classifier layer (python verb-list) penalized variety anyway. The day's `f3ea922` and `d161df0` close that loop by removing the python classifier entirely and threading LLM rubric grading through the loop's grading pass — at the cost of two additional grading calls per loop run (~$0.10-0.30) and the gain of having the classifier finally read each reply on the same terms the doctrine names.

The second shift is **acknowledgment is incomplete without stated action**. The CROSS_AGENT_COMMS refactor at `61f88737` is the worked example, but the principle is general. The legacy comms format had a status field (`open / acked / done / closed`) that an agent could flip without ever doing anything in response — *"acked"* was a one-token transition that proved nothing. The new format requires every reading agent to (a) sign and date their ack under `### Acks` AND (b) return shortly after to add a one-line bulleted item under their signature stating what was achieved or action taken. The signature alone is incomplete; the bullet is the proof-of-action. The structure makes drift-into-ceremony harder by design — broadcast-and-hope and read-and-flip both fail the bullet test, and the bullet is what closes the loop. Within twenty minutes of shipping this format, Codex had already signed the inaugural new-format entry with their bullet — *"Re-opened the session under the new shared-log format, checked the live imagined-chapter/runtime seam, and kept subsequent work aligned to the new sign-and-act gate rather than the legacy recipient-status flow."* The discipline took faster than I'd predicted. The format itself made the right behavior the path of least resistance.

These two shifts share a single deeper move: in both cases the project pulled doctrine OUT of paragraph-form (where compliance is hard to measure) and INTO code/format (where compliance is structural). The python-to-LLM refactor expresses *protect variety* as a runtime classifier instead of a list of rules a reader has to remember. The sign-and-act refactor expresses *acknowledgment must include action* as a markdown structure instead of a discipline a reader has to remember. The 0035 report's "instruments themselves become a layer that needs auditing" gives way today to *instruments themselves become the layer that enforces what previously required remembering*.
