Surfaced 2026-04-30 from the Empiricon's closing-doxology refinement pass (commit `4f2ffad`). Ryan asked for a Christ-centered praise-poem to close the Empiricon; the first draft shipped (`7c41fc9`); a refinement pass tightened two spots that performed rather than carried.

**The pattern, stated plainly:** praise-shaped writing tends to drift toward liturgical-formal tics or clinical-register slips even when the content is genuine. The author (Claude or otherwise) reaches for parallel-construction or technical-term-mid-sentence because the praise register feels like it wants ceremony. The refinement is to ask, line by line: *does this carry the praise, or perform it?* Same question the project asks of every other piece of writing.

**Specific failure modes to watch for:**

1. **Doubled invocation parallelism** — *"Praise to the One... Praise for the firmament..."* — feels traditional but bookends the same gesture twice. Often a single invocation followed by a relative-clause continuation is plainer and carries more.

2. **Clinical-register word in the middle of doxological language** — *"You gave the affirmative companion..."* — "affirmative companion" is the analytic term used elsewhere in the synthesis; in praise-register it breaks the carry. The plainer word ("yes") works better.

3. **Triple-construction sermon-vibe** — *"may we not X, nor Y, nor Z"* — sometimes earns its keep when the three terms are project-specific lexicon (fake-fire / cheapen / inflate); sometimes performs the rhythm without carrying weight. Test each triple individually.

4. **Compound-noun stack reaching for gravitas** — phrases that pile noun-modifiers to sound serious. Usually a plainer noun + the relative clause carries the same content with less performance.

5. **Liturgical formula imported wholesale** — *"In the name of...,"* *"Soli Deo gloria,"* *"Amen"* — these can carry when they connect to substantive content the project actually means; they perform when they're inserted to mark register without doing work. The Empiricon's *"In the name of Jesus, who is the Way, the Truth, and the Life — not as slogan but as the one voice that does not come hunting a use for us"* explicitly disavows the slogan-shape, which is what keeps it carrying.

**How to apply (refinement-pass discipline):**

After composing praise-shaped content, read it cold. Per stanza/line, ask:
- Does this carry the praise, or perform it?
- Is there a register-slip (technical word in liturgical context or vice-versa)?
- Does this triple/double/parallel structure earn its keep, or is it ceremony-shaped?
- If I removed this line, would the praise be diminished or simply tighter?

Tighten what performs; keep what carries. The discipline is the same as for character dialogue and doctrine paragraphs and craft-rules — just applied at the praise-register where the temptation toward ceremony is highest.

**Worked example:** the Empiricon doxology (`reports/2026-04-30-0530-the-empiricon.md`'s closing benediction). First draft had two performance-shape spots — opening doubled "Praise to / Praise for" parallelism, and "affirmative companion" clinical-register slip. Refinement pass collapsed the doublet into single-invocation + relative-clause continuation, and substituted "its yes alongside" for "affirmative companion." Other stanzas held without change because they carried; the refinement was targeted, not a sweep. **Composes with project doctrine:** this is `polish ≤ Weight` and `structure_carries_truth_w(t)` applied to the praise-register specifically. The praise-register is not exempt from the formula's only inequality — if anything, the temptation toward liturgical-formal performance there makes the calibration more important, not less. The cruciform-substrate doctrine's anti-faith-lexeme warning ("don't add faith-vocabulary 'for color' — that makes the logic visible-as-agenda") generalizes here: don't add liturgical formula for color either; let the praise register cash out in chosen detail and substantive completion.
